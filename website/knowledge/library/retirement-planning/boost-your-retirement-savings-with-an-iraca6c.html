    Which IRA could be right for me?

    Which IRA could be right for me?

    E*TRADE from Morgan Stanley

     12/04/24

    An Individual Retirement Account can be a smart, simple way to boost your retirement savings. No matter your financial situation, E*TRADE has an IRA that can help you make progress toward your retirement goals.

    One of the most important steps you can take to make progress towards your retirement goals is to start saving for retirement as early as possible and then keep on making regular contributions.

    Thanks to compound interest, the earlier you start, the less you may have to save each month to reach your retirement goals.

    Your workplace retirement plan is a great place to begin, but if youre already maxing out that accountor you dont have access to oneyou might also consider reserving retirement savings in an Individual Retirement Account (IRA).

    E*TRADE offers a variety of IRAs withno annual feesoraccount minimumsother fees may apply.4The two main types of IRAs are traditional IRAs and Roth IRAs.

    However, there are also some important differences to understand:

    1. Are you eligible to contribute to an IRA?

    There are no restrictions on eligibility to contribute to traditional IRAs but high-income earners cannot contribute to a Roth IRA (or the amount they can contribute may be reduced) if their income exceeds certain levels.

    Traditional IRA

    No restrictions but your eligibility to take a tax deduction for your contributions phases out if you or your spouse is covered by a workplace retirement and your modified adjusted gross income exceeds certain levels.

    Roth IRA

    Your modified adjusted gross income must be below a certain limit to be eligible to contribute.

    For 2025, it must be below:

    • $165,000 for individuals for single individuals
    • $246,000 for married couples filing jointly
    • $10,000 for married couples filing separately

    Your eligibility to contribute phases out if your modified adjusted income for 2025 is between:

    • $150,000 and $165,000 for single individuals
    • $236,000 and $246,000 for married couples filing jointly
    • $0 and $10,000 for married couples filing separately

    2. Tax benefits

    Both offer tax-advantaged ways to save for retirement. For 2024 and 2025, you can contribute a total of $7,000 into your IRA accounts, and those age 50 or older can contribute an extra $1,000 in catch-up contributions. If you own more than one IRA, this limit applies in the aggregate to all the regular contributions made to all your traditional and Roth IRAs for the year.

    Traditional IRA

    Generally, no taxes paid on earnings in the account until the assets are withdrawn from the IRA.

    Depending on income and availability of workplace retirement accounts, contributions may be deductible from your taxable income.

    Roth IRA

    Contributions are not deductible from your taxable income.

    Generally, no taxes paid on earnings in the account. If the qualified distribution1 requirements are met, withdrawals of earnings are not subject to federal income taxes.

    3. Withdrawals and Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

    The rules around how and when you can (or must) access money contributed to the account differ for Traditional and Roth IRAs.

    Withdrawals

    Traditional IRA

    Generally, subject to tax penalties and income taxes if funds are withdrawn before age 59.

    Certain exceptions to the 10% penalty tax may apply.

    Roth IRA

    No tax penalties or income taxes on the withdrawal of your nondeductible contributions, but:

    • Tax penalties and income taxes apply to any earnings withdrawn prior to satisfying the qualified distribution requirements.

    Distributions of earnings made after you satisfy the qualified distribution requirements are income tax and penalty tax free.

    Distributions of amounts that were converted to a Roth IRA may be subject to a 10% tax penalty if withdrawn within five years per the IRS 5-year rule. Be sure to review with your tax advisor.

    RMDs

    Traditional IRA

    Withdrawals must begin once you reach RMD age (currently, age 73).

    Roth IRA

    No requirement to withdraw funds during the lifetime of the original account holder (but after-death RMD rules apply to the account holders beneficiaries).

    • Stocks, exchange-traded funds (ETFs), mutual funds, bonds
    • Futures and options

    • Are you older than 59 ? E*TRADE Complete IRA provides flexible access to cash with free checking, online bill pay and an ATM/debit card.2,3

    • Do you have a child earning income? Our IRA for minors is for those under 18 with earned income.
